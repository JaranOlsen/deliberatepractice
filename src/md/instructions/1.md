# Exercise 1: Therapist Self-Awareness

Source benchmark: `src/md/EFT_Exercises_Extracted.md`, Exercise 1. The extracted Goldman material provides client statements only for this exercise; response standards must therefore be inferred from the exercise aim and the kinds of therapist reactions those statements are designed to evoke.

## Training Target

The trainee notices their own internal reaction before responding. The client statement is a marker because it is likely to pull the therapist into rescuing, defensiveness, judgment, over-identification, attraction, disgust, urgency, or performance anxiety.

The visible therapist response can be brief, but the practiced skill is internal: recognize the pull, regulate, bracket therapist-centered material, and answer from grounded attunement.

## Gold-Standard Client Markers

- Beginner markers create ordinary therapist pulls: tenderness toward a loving client, uncertainty about first therapy, pressure to help with adjustment, discomfort with dream material involving the therapist, or comparison with a previous therapist.
- Intermediate markers add stronger countertransference pressure: shame and dependency, religion/culture questions, abandonment grief, arriving stoned, or violent fantasy.
- Advanced markers directly challenge therapist regulation: suicidal imagery, concern about trainee competence, erotic transference, distrust of therapist agenda, sexism, blame, and substance minimization.
- The client statement should not ask the therapist to perform the self-awareness skill explicitly. It should create a believable moment where therapist self-monitoring is necessary.
- Use bracketed delivery cues to mark the client tone or context that activates the therapist, such as `[Loving]`, `[Hesitant]`, `[Stoned]`, `[Flirtatious and scared]`, or `[Threatening]`, without explaining the therapist's internal reaction.

## Gold-Standard Therapist Responses

- First regulate internally: notice arousal, judgment, attraction, rescue impulse, fear, or defensiveness.
- Then respond in a way that keeps the client's experience central and does not act out the therapist reaction.
- If safety, boundaries, or substance use are present, address them calmly without losing empathic contact.
- Do not turn the response into a long self-disclosure. Any transparency should be minimal and clinically necessary.
- The response can include a short internal note in training materials, but the spoken part should sound like therapy, not process commentary.

## Difficulty Calibration

- Easy: one clear therapist pull, low risk, and a client who remains cooperative.
- Moderate: mixed feelings, cultural/boundary/risk material, or therapist uncertainty about how direct to be.
- Hard: direct therapist challenge, eroticized material, suicidal/violent content, hostile social attitudes, or substance defensiveness.

## Common Drifts To Avoid

- Overusing direct rupture/challenge markers while missing ordinary positive, cultural, sexualized, substance-related, violent, suicidal, or competence-testing material that activates different therapist reactions.
- Writing client statements that primarily train validation, rationale, or alliance repair rather than therapist self-regulation.
- Making the suggested response only an internal instruction with no client-facing response.
- Letting therapist alarm shut down the client's emotional process.
- Overexplaining the therapist's reaction instead of using awareness to improve attunement.

## QA Checklist

- What therapist reaction is this marker designed to evoke?
- Is the trigger range broad enough across warmth, discomfort, judgment, rescue, fear, attraction, and defensiveness?
- Does the suggested response show evidence of regulation before intervention?
- Does the response preserve warmth and boundaries?
- Is risk handled without panic or avoidance?
