# Exercise 8: Empathic Conjectures

Source benchmark: `src/md/EFT_Exercises_Extracted.md`, Exercise 8.

## Training Target

The trainee offers a soft, tentative guess about a near-surface feeling, meaning, or need that the client has not fully named. The intervention reaches slightly beyond empathic understanding while remaining anchored in observable cues.

## Gold-Standard Client Markers

- Beginner markers show an unspoken feeling close to the surface: hurt behind anger, fear behind avoidance, shame behind withdrawal.
- Intermediate markers include ambivalence, relational conflict, or a puzzling reaction that suggests a deeper emotional logic.
- Advanced markers involve strong defenses, denial, trauma, identity conflict, or rupture risk where the hidden feeling is plausible but not explicit.
- Good markers contain enough evidence for a guess: tone, contradiction, avoidance, sudden shift, body cue, or repeated theme.
- Bracketed delivery cues may supply observable evidence for the conjecture, such as `[Dismissive]`, `[Voice shaking]`, or `[Quiet and ashamed]`; they should not name the guessed feeling directly unless the client is already showing it.

## Gold-Standard Therapist Responses

- Signal tentativeness: "I wonder if," "could it be," "maybe part of you."
- Name the possible underlying feeling, meaning, or need.
- Stay close enough that the client can correct you without feeling invaded.
- Keep the guess emotionally useful, not clever.
- If the guess is wrong, the response should still help clarify the client's actual experience.

## Difficulty Calibration

- Easy: the conjecture is only a small step beyond the client's words.
- Moderate: the conjecture links two parts of a conflict or names a less obvious primary emotion.
- Hard: the conjecture touches defensive organization, shame, trauma, dependency, or distrust.

## Common Drifts To Avoid

- Sounding certain or mind-reading.
- Leaping several steps beyond the evidence.
- Asking a disguised question instead of offering a conjecture.
- Repeating the client's explicit feeling without adding the near-surface guess.

## QA Checklist

- What evidence supports the conjecture?
- Is the response tentative enough?
- Does it name something not fully named by the client?
- Could the client safely disagree?
