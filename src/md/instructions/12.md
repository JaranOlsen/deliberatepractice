# Exercise 12: Addressing Ruptures and Facilitating Repair

Source benchmark: `src/md/EFT_Exercises_Extracted.md`, Exercise 12.

## Training Target

The trainee receives rupture feedback openly, validates the client's impact, owns the therapist contribution where relevant, clarifies the frame without defensiveness, and collaborates on what would help the relationship/work now.

## Gold-Standard Client Markers

- Beginner markers include worries that the therapist is bored, fear of feeling-focused work because of past harm, concern about trainee experience, or belief that the client is doing therapy wrong.
- Intermediate markers include resistance to a therapy focus, anger about a therapist misattunement, concerns about age/culture/experience mismatch, or fear the therapist is not interested.
- Advanced markers include stern criticism that therapy is not working, fear of wasting time, or sexual/romantic suspicion after therapist questions.
- A good marker places the therapeutic relationship or therapy process itself at stake.

## Gold-Standard Therapist Responses

- Thank or appreciate the client for bringing the concern.
- Validate the impact before explaining intention.
- Own the therapist contribution when there was a misattunement.
- Apologize clearly when appropriate.
- Clarify boundaries or therapist stance only after receiving the client's experience.
- Invite continued feedback and collaborate on what would help now.

## Difficulty Calibration

- Easy: concern is direct but not hostile; repair can be straightforward.
- Moderate: client names a specific hurt or mismatch; therapist may need ownership.
- Hard: client questions the whole therapy, the therapist's motives, or the sexual/professional boundary.

## Common Drifts To Avoid

- Narrowing rupture markers to pacing or technique complaints only; include concerns about boredom, competence/status mismatch, previous therapy harm, the therapy not working, and professional/sexual boundaries.
- Defending intention before receiving impact.
- Turning repair into treatment rationale too quickly.
- Reassuring the client without owning the relational moment.
- Moving back to technique before the repair lands.

## QA Checklist

- What rupture or relational concern is the client naming?
- Is the relationship or therapy process genuinely at stake, rather than only a preference about technique?
- Does the response receive impact first?
- Is ownership/apology included where relevant?
- Does the response ask or invite what would help repair the work now?
