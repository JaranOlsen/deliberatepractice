# Exercise 7: Empathic Evocations

Source benchmark: `src/md/EFT_Exercises_Extracted.md`, Exercise 7.

## Training Target

The trainee offers vivid, sensory, imagistic language that helps a near-surface feeling come alive. The response should heighten felt contact without becoming theatrical or imported.

## Gold-Standard Client Markers

- Goldman benchmark examples sometimes include client imagery. For this app, treat those examples as source inspiration, not the default marker style.
- The strongest deliberate-practice marker is often emotionally charged but comparatively flat, factual, detached, cognitive, or symptom-focused.
- Beginner markers should give a clear emotional hook while leaving the evocative image for the therapist.
- Intermediate markers may include mixed emotion, bodily cues, or relational conflict, but should still avoid handing the full metaphor to the trainee.
- Advanced markers may sound defended, numb, factual, intellectualized, or guarded while carrying high emotional pressure underneath.

## Gold-Standard Therapist Responses

- Offer a brief image, metaphor, sensory phrase, or embodied description that fits the client's experience.
- Stay emotionally congruent with the marker; do not overdramatize.
- Make the feeling more alive while preserving the client's agency.
- Use tentative phrasing when the image is bolder.
- The response should help the client feel "yes, that is what it is like."

## Difficulty Calibration

- Easy: one emotion and an obvious sensory direction.
- Moderate: mixed feeling, guardedness, or a less obvious image.
- Hard: trauma, shame, denial, distrust, or numbness where the evocation must be powerful but careful.

## Common Drifts To Avoid

- Letting the client statement already contain the best metaphor.
- Writing therapist responses that are pretty but not clinically attuned.
- Confusing evocation with conjecture by guessing hidden feelings.
- Confusing evocation with exploration by asking the client to do all the sensing.

## QA Checklist

- Does the client marker leave image-making room for the trainee?
- What feeling should the image heighten?
- Is the image grounded in the client's actual words, context, or body cues?
- Would the response deepen contact without pushing outside the window of tolerance?
