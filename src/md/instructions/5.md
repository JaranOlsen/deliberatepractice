# Exercise 5: Providing Treatment Rationale for Emotion-Focused Therapy

Source benchmark: `src/md/EFT_Exercises_Extracted.md`, Exercise 5.

## Training Target

The trainee explains why emotion-focused work is relevant, useful, and safe enough to try. The response joins the client's concern first, then offers a plain-language rationale tied to the client's fear, goal, or hesitation.

## Gold-Standard Client Markers

- Beginner markers ask how therapy works, question the point of feelings, or prefer practical coping.
- Intermediate markers include skepticism about chair work, fear of being overwhelmed, doubts about emotion, or concern that focusing on pain will make things worse.
- Advanced markers include entrenched avoidance, trauma-related fear, shame about needing help, strong distrust of therapy, or refusal of experiential tasks.
- Good markers often sound like resistance, but clinically they are requests for orientation, safety, and collaboration.

## Gold-Standard Therapist Responses

- Start by acknowledging the client's concern.
- Explain the EFT logic in everyday words: feelings carry information, avoided feelings keep returning, and contacted feelings can change.
- Connect the rationale directly to this client's presenting problem or desired outcome.
- Clarify pacing and safety: the client will not be abandoned in overwhelming affect.
- Keep it brief enough to support engagement, not lecture.

## Difficulty Calibration

- Easy: curiosity or mild skepticism.
- Moderate: fear that emotion work is useless, unsafe, or embarrassing.
- Hard: active refusal, trauma history, severe shame, or alliance strain around the method.

## Common Drifts To Avoid

- Giving theory-heavy explanations that sound like teaching.
- Arguing the client out of resistance.
- Offering reassurance without explaining why the intervention helps.
- Forgetting to link the rationale to the client's own goals.

## QA Checklist

- What concern is the client raising about the work?
- Does the response explain why this emotional focus helps this client?
- Does it include pacing or safety when relevant?
- Does it invite collaboration rather than compliance?
