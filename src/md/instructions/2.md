# Exercise 2: Empathic Understanding

Source benchmark: `src/md/EFT_Exercises_Extracted.md`, Exercise 2.

## Training Target

The trainee offers an accurate, concise reflection of the client's explicitly available emotional meaning. The response conveys "I understand what this is like for you" without advising, interpreting, questioning, validating in context, or trying to heighten emotion.

## Gold-Standard Client Markers

- Beginner markers contain a clear feeling and a simple situation: grief, optimism, stress, or loss.
- Intermediate markers include more context, repetitive conflict, worry, relational hurt, anger, or confusion, but the main felt meaning is still reachable from what the client says.
- Advanced markers are denser or more existential: numbness, identity pain, regret, unresolved family hurt, or a painful life pattern.
- The marker should give enough emotional content for a reflection, but it should not require the therapist to guess hidden material.
- Bracketed delivery cues should name the explicit tone or affect already available in the statement, such as `[Sad]`, `[Worried]`, `[Flat]`, or `[Very sad]`; do not use them to add hidden meaning.

## Gold-Standard Therapist Responses

- Stay close to the client's words and emotional meaning.
- Keep it short: usually one sentence, occasionally two.
- Reflect both feeling and personal significance when the client gives enough information.
- Use natural feeling language rather than theory words.
- No advice, no rationale, no question, no reassurance, no metaphor-heavy evocation.

## Difficulty Calibration

- Easy: one emotion, obvious target, low ambiguity.
- Moderate: more story detail or mixed feeling, but still enough explicit emotion to reflect.
- Hard: complex or global pain where the reflection must organize the experience without adding interpretation.

## Common Drifts To Avoid

- Making beginner markers too vivid, metaphorical, or case-formulated; include ordinary sadness, stress, readiness, and simple loss.
- Turning the response into validation by saying why the feeling makes sense.
- Turning the response into exploration by asking what happens inside.
- Turning the response into conjecture by naming feelings not shown.
- Adding "and maybe..." guesses when the core task is accurate mirroring.

## QA Checklist

- Is the reflected feeling explicitly present in the marker?
- Could the client plausibly say "yes, that is what I meant"?
- Is the response concise enough for a beginner to imitate?
- Does it avoid advice, questions, and explanation?
