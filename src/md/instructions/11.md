# Exercise 11: Marker Recognition and Chair Work Task Setup

Source benchmark: `src/md/EFT_Exercises_Extracted.md`, Exercise 11.

## Training Target

The trainee recognizes a chair-work task marker and sets up the appropriate experiential task clearly enough to begin. The response does not need to complete chair work; it must identify the process, offer a rationale, obtain collaboration, arrange the chairs, and give the first concrete instruction.

## Gold-Standard Client Markers

- Self-evaluative split: harsh self-attack, self-blame, shame, contempt, or worry that attacks and weakens the self.
- Self-interruptive split: blocking tears, suppressing anger, shutting down, numbing, tightening, or stopping expression.
- Unfinished business: unresolved pain, anger, fear, longing, guilt, or unmet need toward a significant other.
- Beginner markers clearly show one of these three processes.
- Intermediate markers add grief, guilt, numbness, divorce, death, or unresolved regret.
- Advanced markers include trauma, Holocaust/family legacy, betrayal imagery, catastrophizing/worry splits, or fear of expressing anger even in imagination.

## Gold-Standard Therapist Responses

- Name the marker in ordinary language: "you are really coming down on yourself," "you tighten and stop the tears," or "there is unfinished business with her."
- Give a brief experiential rationale: the task helps the client become aware of the critic/interrupter or speak directly to the significant other.
- Ask permission or invite collaboration.
- Set up the correct chairs: two-chair for self-evaluative/self-interruptive splits; empty chair for unfinished business.
- Give a specific first turn: tell yourself the criticism, show how you stop the feeling, or speak directly to the imagined other.

## Difficulty Calibration

- Easy: obvious marker and low ambiguity about task type.
- Moderate: mixed guilt/grief/numbness, stronger shame, or more careful permission needed.
- Hard: trauma, intense anger, moral injury, catastrophizing, or client resistance to the imagined dialogue.

## Common Drifts To Avoid

- Writing client statements that already announce the task with neat labels like "a part of me," "the critic," "the protector," or "this is unfinished business." The marker should be recognizable from ordinary client speech.
- Offering empathic reflection without setting up the task.
- Choosing the wrong task type for the marker.
- Explaining chair work abstractly but not giving concrete first instructions.
- Pushing the client into chair work without consent or readiness.

## QA Checklist

- Which marker is present: self-evaluative, self-interruptive, unfinished business, or worry split?
- Does the client statement sound like something the client would say, rather than a disguised instruction to do chair work?
- Is the proposed task the correct chair task?
- Does the response include consent, setup, and first-turn instruction?
- Is arousal/readiness considered for difficult material?
