# Exercise 10: Self-Disclosure

Source benchmark: `src/md/EFT_Exercises_Extracted.md`, Exercise 10.

## Training Target

The trainee uses brief, boundaried therapist transparency when it serves the client's process. The disclosure clarifies the therapist's stance, repairs uncertainty, or validates the client's impact, then returns focus to the client.

## Gold-Standard Client Markers

- Beginner markers include direct questions about therapy or therapist identity, fear of being seen as "crazy," faith/culture questions, or feeling vulnerable with the therapist.
- Intermediate markers include noticing therapist behavior, worrying the therapist is bored, feeling uncomfortable in therapy, or grief where therapist emotional resonance may help.
- Advanced markers include repeated lateness, attractiveness questions, accusations about pity, doubts about therapist experience, or other boundary/competence challenges.
- Good markers make therapist neutrality potentially evasive; some transparent response would likely strengthen trust or clarify the frame.
- Bracketed cues should often include therapy context or interpersonal delivery, such as `[Beginning of first session]`, `[Curious]`, `[Very uncomfortable]`, `[Arrives late]`, or `[Angry, clenching fists]`, because the disclosure decision depends on how the question lands.

## Gold-Standard Therapist Responses

- Answer the relevant part honestly and briefly.
- Keep boundaries clear, especially around attractiveness, romance, competence, religion, or personal history.
- Do not use disclosure to meet the therapist's needs.
- Return focus to the client with a clinically meaningful question or reflection.
- Own therapist behavior or impact when relevant.

## Difficulty Calibration

- Easy: low-risk personal or process question.
- Moderate: therapist behavior is named, or the client worries about therapist interest/care.
- Hard: boundary-testing, attraction, competence challenge, chronic lateness, or shame-sensitive appearance concerns.

## Common Drifts To Avoid

- Giving biography when a simple stance disclosure is enough.
- Avoiding the question so the client feels managed.
- Reassuring without transparency.
- Failing to return the focus to the client's experience.

## QA Checklist

- What does the client need to know from the therapist?
- Is the disclosure brief and clinically relevant?
- Are boundaries clear?
- Does the response return to the client's process?
